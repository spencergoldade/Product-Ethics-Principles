## Preface

These principles are a practitioner's reference for product designers, product managers, and the strategists and DEI leaders who work alongside them. They are intended to function as heuristics — tools for evaluating existing products and guiding the design of new ones — in the tradition of Jakob Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics and Bruce Tognazzini's First Principles of Interaction Design. Where those frameworks focus on functional usability and interaction quality, these principles focus on the moral, ethical, and social impact of what we build.

The framework rests on a foundational observation: the difference between a product that claims to be good for the world and one that actually is mirrors the difference between performative and structural DEI: the former makes declarations; the latter makes design decisions, measures outcomes, and accepts accountability. Declarations cannot be violated. Design decisions can. Every principle in this document is written to be violable and to define failure as clearly as success.

These principles can be applied in three modes:



1. **Design mode:** During product creation, use these principles to evaluate proposed features, flows, and defaults before they ship.
2. **Review mode:** Apply these principles as a regular audit heuristic for existing products, at each major release, and no less than annually.
3. **Governance mode:** Use these principles as a framework for establishing team norms, design critique criteria, and escalation standards.

And if you’re using AI to help you with any of those things it gets even easier, as you add specialized rules and skills to guide how the AI approaches tasks and what it suggests.

**Severity Levels**



1. **Foundational:** Violations actively harm users. These are non-negotiable and represent the ethical floor. No business justification overrides a Foundational violation.
2. **Structural:** Violations limit who can benefit from the system, reducing its reach, equity, and long-term integrity. Structural violations are serious and should be addressed systematically.
3. **Aspirational:** The system could do more good than it currently does. Aspirational principles represent the ceiling toward which good products should continuously move.


## The Principles at a Glance

A quick view of each principle, followed by detailed breakdowns in the next section.

**1.  Accessibility:** *A system is not finished until it can be used by people with a full range of abilities, devices, connection qualities, languages, and levels of technical familiarity.*

**2.  Diversity:** *A system actively designs for, and is shaped by, users of diverse personal identities, backgrounds, languages, cultures, and perspectives; it does not merely invite them.*

**3.  Equity:** *A system removes structural barriers that prevent users from different starting points, whether economic, geographic, educational, or demographic, from having a fair opportunity to participate in and benefit from the system.*

**4.  Inclusion:** *The system's structures, processes, defaults, and features are designed to enable all users to participate fully, not merely to gain entry.*

**5.  Belonging:** *No user should be systematically made to feel unwelcome, othered, or dehumanized by the design, defaults, content, moderation, or community norms of the system.*

**6.  Privacy:** *The system collects only what it needs, uses data only for the purposes users consented to, actively protects users from the downstream consequences of data that exists about them, and treats user data as a trust obligation, not an asset.*

**7.  Security:** *The system protects user data and system integrity from unauthorized access, exploitation, and breach; security is a prerequisite for any system that handles user information, not a feature to be added post-launch.*

**8.  Safety:** *The system designs to prevent and mitigate psychological, emotional, mental, and physical harm to its users; proactively protects vulnerable populations; and provides accessible, effective means for users to seek help, report harm, and obtain remedy when the system fails.*

**9.  Representation & Stigma:** *The system does not perpetuate, amplify, or introduce harmful representations of people; it actively examines its own language, imagery, defaults, and content for dehumanizing or stigmatizing patterns, and maintains a process for identifying and correcting them.*

**10.  Algorithmic Accountability:** *The system is transparent about how algorithmic decisions are made, enables users to understand and contest decisions that affect them, and proactively tests for and remediates disparate outcomes across demographic groups.*

**11.  User Health:** *The system respects and supports the physical, psychological, and behavioral health of its users; it does not employ persuasive technology techniques to override user judgment, manufacture artificial needs, or optimize for engagement at the expense of wellbeing.*

**12.  Autonomy & Agency:** *The system enables users to make meaningful, informed choices about their experience; it does not use psychological manipulation to override user judgment; and it provides users with genuine, accessible control over their data, settings, and participation.*

**13.  Honesty & Truth:** *The system does not deceive users through its design, language, or content; it discloses algorithmic curation and AI-generated content; it does not enable or amplify the spread of false information; and it respects users' epistemic autonomy— their capacity to form independent, well-informed judgments.*

**14.  False Obsolescence:** *The system does not artificially shorten its own useful life, manufacture user dependency that traps users, or design features or hardware to fail or become obsolete before their natural end of life.*

**15.  Economic Justice:** *The system does not exploit information asymmetry, psychological vulnerability, or market power to extract disproportionate value from users; it is transparent about its commercial incentives; and it does not engineer financial harm as a product feature.*

**16.  Environmental Sustainability:** *The system minimizes its ecological footprint across its full lifecycle, from the energy cost of computation to the materials cost of hardware to the waste generated at end of life, and does not make false or misleading claims about its environmental impact.*

**17.  Labor Ethics:** *The system's ethical responsibility extends to the people who build, operate, and support it; the team behind the product has the same claim to dignity, safety, and fair compensation as the users in front of it.*

**18.  Civic Responsibility:** *The system acknowledges and takes responsibility for its aggregate effects on democratic institutions, social cohesion, and public discourse; it does not design for engagement in ways that predictably corrode civic trust; and it does not enable the weaponization of its infrastructure against democratic participation or civic safety.*

### **1.** Accessibility


    *A system is not finished until it can be used by people with a full range of abilities, devices, connection qualities, languages, and levels of technical familiarity.*

**Severity: Foundational**

**Intent**

Accessibility is not a feature. It is the baseline cost of building something meant to be used by humans, who are, by definition, varied in ability, circumstance, and context. A product that is inaccessible to some people has not been designed for all people; it has been designed for its creators. The most common inaccessibility failure is not a specific technical omission but a cultural assumption: that the user is like the designer.

The accessibility obligation extends well beyond visual disability and screen reader support. It encompasses sensory, cognitive, and motor disabilities; situational disabilities (bright sunlight, one-handed use, fatigue, noisy environments); age-related changes in vision, hearing, and motor control; and infrastructure barriers including bandwidth, device capability, and data cost. Cognitive accessibility, supporting users with dyslexia, ADHD, anxiety, autism spectrum conditions, and traumatic brain injury, is the most prevalent accessibility need by population and the most consistently under-addressed.

**In Practice**



* Meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA for all user-facing interfaces as the legal and ethical floor; pursue AAA where feasible.
* Conduct accessibility audits with automated tools (axe, Lighthouse, WAVE) and with disabled users in structured testing.
* Provide multiple content modalities: text, audio, captions, and where appropriate, simplified versions.
* Design for keyboard-only navigation, screen reader compatibility, and voice input.
* Test on lower-end devices and constrained connection speeds before launch.
* Apply plain language standards: active voice, short sentences, common words.
* Never use color as the sole means of conveying information.
* Make all timed interactions user-adjustable.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Accessibility as afterthought: Retrofitting accessibility post-launch is far more expensive and less effective than designing for it from the start.
* Screen reader theater: Adding ARIA labels that satisfy automated scans but do not produce a coherent screen reader experience.
* The able-bodied assumption: User research that never includes participants with disabilities, validating designs only against a narrow demographic.
* Color-only affordances: Red/green status indicators with no shape, text, or pattern distinction.
* Motion by default: Parallax, animations, and auto-playing video without a mechanism to pause or disable, triggering vestibular disorders.
* The accessibility mode trap: A separate, inferior version of the product for disabled users instead of unified accessible design.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

In Domino's Pizza v. Robles (US Supreme Court, 2019), Domino's argued ADA requirements did not apply to their website. The court disagreed. Accessibility debt became legal liability, establishing website accessibility as a civil rights obligation, not a best practice.<sup>1</sup>

**Connections**

Accessibility intersects with Equity (infrastructure access), Belonging (whether users feel capable of engaging), and Labor Ethics (accessibility work must be resourced, not volunteered by disabled employees).

**For the Builder**

Accessible products come from teams that include disabled designers, researchers, and engineers. Accessibility research must include participants with disabilities, paid at the same rate as any research participant. Accessibility work must be resourced as infrastructure, not treated as a sprint-end cleanup task.


### 2.  Diversity


    *A system actively designs for, and is shaped by, users of diverse personal identities, backgrounds, languages, cultures, and perspectives; it does not merely invite them.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

Invitation without structural design is theater. A product that announces it welcomes all users while encoding a single culture's assumptions in its defaults, name fields, gender options, date formats, illustrations, and language does not welcome diverse users, it tolerates them. Tolerated users encounter friction at every point where the product assumes they are someone else.

Diversity in products has two equally important dimensions: diversity of users (who can access and benefit from the system) and diversity of builders (whose perspectives shaped the system). The most robust evidence for diverse product outcomes comes from diverse product teams. Homogeneous teams build homogeneous products with consistent, predictable blind spots, not because of bad intent, but because design inevitably encodes the assumptions of its designers.

**In Practice**



* Default content (E.g. illustrations, example names, stock imagery) should represent the diversity of the intended user base, not add representation as decoration over a homogeneous default.
* Form fields must accommodate genuine human variety: names that allow non-Western naming conventions, gender options that are not binary unless legally required, phone fields for international formats.
* Localization means more than translation: calendar systems, date formats, address conventions, and culturally appropriate metaphors all require intentional design.
* User research and testing panels should reflect demographic diversity; track this explicitly.
* Evaluate recommendation and content-surfacing systems for whether they amplify or reduce diversity of perspective.

**Anti-Patterns**



* The universal design myth: Assuming a design can be neutral and universal, when in practice it encodes the perspectives of whoever built it.
* Representation as decoration: Adding diverse faces to hero images while leaving all structural assumptions unchanged.
* Translation without localization: Translating UI strings while all UX patterns and support resources remain culturally specific to one context.
* The "real user" assumption: Research pools that skew toward young, educated, Western, able-bodied, English-speaking users, producing designs that confidently serve that group.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

In 2020, Twitter's image cropping algorithm was found to consistently center white faces over Black faces when images contained both. The algorithm had been trained without adequate review of disparate outcomes. Users noticed immediately.<sup>21</sup> In 2015, Google Photos' image labeling tagged photos of Black people with terms used for animals, a catastrophic failure of diverse review in the development process. <sup>2, 3</sup>

**Connections**

Diversity intersects with Inclusion (structural conditions for diverse participation), Equity (removing access barriers), Belonging (diverse users feeling genuinely valued), Algorithmic Accountability (systems that amplify or reduce demographic representation), and Labor Ethics (diverse builders as prerequisite for diverse products).

**For the Builder**

Team diversity is a product quality issue, not only an HR issue. Establish explicit targets for demographic representation in user research panels. Track the demographic composition of design critique participants, beta testers, and advisory groups. Audit default content at each major release.


### 3.  Equity


    *A system removes structural barriers that prevent users from different starting points, whether economic, geographic, educational, or demographic, from having a fair opportunity to participate in and benefit from the system.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

Equality gives everyone the same thing. Equity gives everyone what they need to have a fair chance. Treating all users identically regardless of their circumstances (the equality trap) produces systematically unequal outcomes, because users do not begin from equal positions.

The equity obligation operates across several dimensions: economic equity (can people afford to use this?), geographic and infrastructure equity (does it work as well in rural Saskatchewan as in San Francisco?), educational and literacy equity (does it require knowledge or fluency not universally available?), algorithmic equity (does the system produce systematically different outcomes across demographic groups?), and historical equity (does the design perpetuate historical inequalities encoded in its training data or structural assumptions?).

**In Practice**



* Evaluate minimum hardware and connectivity requirements against the economic profile of the intended user base.
* Assess whether core safety-related features are accessible without payment.
* Test product performance on low-end devices and 3G-equivalent connections.
* Audit algorithmic outputs for disparate impact across demographic groups before launch and on an ongoing basis.
* Provide progressive disclosure: the system should work with the knowledge users have and build from there.
* Identify the structural 'default user' of the product and explicitly test with users who do not match that profile.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Safety behind a paywall: Placing content moderation reporting tools, privacy controls, or safety features in paid tiers while free users have reduced protection.
* The equality trap: A single, identical onboarding experience regardless of prior knowledge or technical context.
* Algorithmic amplification of historical inequity: Recommendation systems trained on historical engagement data that systematically surface content from historically dominant demographics.
* Free as extraction: Products with no monetary cost that extract value through behavioral profiling, where the equity cost is borne by users least equipped to understand it.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

COVID-19 vaccine scheduling portals in the US required stable internet, modern devices, and high digital literacy, systematically disadvantaging elderly, rural, and lower-income populations who were simultaneously at highest medical risk. The product assumed a user it did not have, producing measurable health equity harm. <sup>4, 5</sup>

**Connections**

Equity intersects with Accessibility (infrastructure access as an accessibility issue), Economic Justice (pricing models as equity decisions), Algorithmic Accountability (disparate algorithmic outcomes), and Belonging (whether users who face structural barriers feel welcomed despite them).

**For the Builder**

Equity audits, structured reviews of who is included and excluded by the product's structural assumptions, should be conducted before major launches and at regular intervals. These are not user research exercises; they are structural analyses identifying users who are not present because the product was not built for them.


### 4.  Inclusion


    *The system's structures, processes, defaults, and features are designed to enable all users to participate fully, not merely to gain entry.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

Access is not inclusion. A door that opens for everyone is not an inclusive design if the room inside requires everyone to stand, and some people use wheelchairs. Inclusion is the difference between being technically permitted to participate and being structurally enabled to do so.

Inclusion is an objective, structural condition, distinct from Belonging, which is the subjective feeling of being valued. A system can be technically inclusive while generating experiences that make some users feel unwanted. Inclusion asks: for every feature, workflow, default, and content decision, whose participation is this designed to enable, and whose is it designed to merely permit?

**In Practice**



* Audit all core features for parity across user tiers, device types, and demographic groups.
* Ensure feedback, reporting, and contribution mechanisms are accessible to users with limited technical literacy, limited bandwidth, and non-English languages.
* Review all system copy, labels, error messages, and help content for inclusive language.
* Establish explicit standards for what defaults encode and who they serve; defaults should be explainable and easily changed.
* Moderation appeal processes and crisis resources must be available in all languages the product operates in.

**Anti-Patterns**



* The reduced-function mobile: Mobile users, disproportionately lower-income and from developing markets, receiving a systematically degraded experience.
* English-only safety: Moderation appeals and safety reporting available only in English in a multilingual product.
* Contribution through friction: Feedback and feature request mechanisms requiring accounts, technical knowledge, or specific device capabilities, filtering to power users.
* Invisible defaults: Settings that significantly affect user experience, data sharing, notification frequency, content filtering, never surfaced or explained to users.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

The persistent gap between iOS and Android feature parity in many major products, where Android users, disproportionately represented in lower-income demographics, receive features later and with more defects, is a structural inclusion failure that is widely normalized across the industry. <sup>6</sup>

**Connections**

Inclusion intersects with Accessibility (structural enabling for disabled users), Belonging (structural inclusion as the foundation of subjective belonging), Diversity (diverse users require inclusive structures to fully participate), and Autonomy (users must be able to meaningfully configure their inclusion conditions).

**For the Builder**

Review product roadmaps for inclusion debt: features built for one demographic that have never been extended to others. Establish and publish a policy on mobile-web feature parity. Apply inclusive language standards to all product copy, including error messages, onboarding, and system-generated notifications.


### 5.  Belonging


    *No user should be systematically made to feel unwelcome, othered, or dehumanized by the design, defaults, content, moderation, or community norms of the system.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

Belonging is the subjective experience of being genuinely valued, not merely tolerated. This principle is not about comfort. It is about freedom from systematic exclusion. Belonging does not require the absence of challenge or disagreement; it requires the absence of design-enabled dehumanization: experiences where the system's defaults, moderation asymmetries, or community norms communicate to specific users that they do not belong.

The belonging obligation is primarily about architecture, not content. Any sufficiently large community will contain bad actors. The question is whether the system is structured to protect its most vulnerable members. Moderation systems, reporting mechanisms, and enforcement patterns are all belonging infrastructure, and they are systematically under-resourced in most products because their failure does not appear in engagement metrics until it is severe enough to generate press coverage.

**In Practice**



* Community guidelines must define unacceptable behaviour specifically, not vaguely. 'Be respectful' is not a standard. Named prohibited behaviours with defined consequences are.
* Moderation and reporting mechanisms must be equally accessible to all users and must include a meaningful appeals process.
* Conduct regular audits of moderation outcomes disaggregated by demographic group. Disparate outcomes are a belonging failure even when no individual decision is intentionally discriminatory.
* Provide users with meaningful controls: muting, blocking, content filtering, and privacy settings are belonging infrastructure.
* Evaluate onboarding and default illustrations for whether they assume a specific demographic.

**Anti-Patterns**



* "We don't tolerate hate" with no enforcement: Community guidelines that exist to signal values but are not enforced.
* Asymmetric moderation: Systems that flag AAVE or minority community self-description as "toxic" while missing equivalent majority-community violations.
* Reporting theater: A content reporting mechanism that generates automated responses and no meaningful follow-through.
* Defaulting to real names: Real-name policies that endanger LGBTQ+ users, domestic violence survivors, activists, and journalists for whom pseudonymity is a safety requirement.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

Facebook's real-name policy was consistently applied to drag performers, trans users, Indigenous users, and domestic violence survivors, while being effectively unenforced for most users. The policy encoded Western legal naming conventions as universal, communicating clearly to affected communities that the platform was not designed for them. <sup>7, 8</sup>

**Connections**

Belonging intersects with Inclusion (structural conditions enabling belonging), Safety (psychological safety as a belonging precondition), Diversity (whether the community's composition reflects genuine welcome), and Representation & Stigma (whether system content signals welcome or othering).

**For the Builder**

Conduct community experience audits with participants from underrepresented groups, structured safety walkthroughs of the product's reporting, moderation, and community mechanisms. Do not assume belonging; test for it.


### 6.  Privacy


    *The system collects only what it needs, uses data only for the purposes users consented to, actively protects users from the downstream consequences of data that exists about them, and treats user data as a trust obligation, not an asset.*

**Severity: Foundational**

**Intent**

Privacy is not achieved at the moment of consent. A clicked "I agree" is necessary but insufficient. The full privacy obligation requires: data minimization (collect only what is needed for the declared purpose); purpose limitation (data collected for one purpose must not be used for another without renewed consent); third-party accountability (consent to a product is not consent to its entire data-broker supply chain); inference accountability (behavioral patterns that reveal sensitive characteristics are sensitive data, regardless of label); children's data protection (users include minors, whose data has heightened legal and ethical protections); breach accountability (users must be notified promptly and honestly when data is compromised); and surveillance-by-design scrutiny (features that enable one user to monitor another have particular obligations in intimate partner violence contexts).

**In Practice**



* Conduct a data inventory before launch: for every piece of data collected, document what it is, why it is needed, how long it is retained, and who can access it.
* Apply data minimization as a design constraint: the default is to not collect. Collecting requires justification.
* Write privacy policies in plain language. If a user cannot understand your privacy policy, it does not constitute informed consent.
* Provide granular consent options, not binary all-or-nothing agreements.
* Make data deletion easy, complete, and permanent.
* Evaluate all third-party integrations for data-sharing implications.
* Test location sharing and activity visibility features for intimate partner violence misuse scenarios before launch.
* Establish a breach response protocol before a breach occurs.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Dark pattern consent: Pre-ticked checkboxes, consent bundles mixing required and optional data, flows designed to minimize decline rates.
* Privacy policy as legal shield: A 30-page document written to insulate the company from liability rather than to inform users.
* The analytics exception: Collecting and sharing detailed behavioral data under the label "analytics" while treating it as categorically distinct from "personal data."
* Surveillance features without safety design: Location sharing and read receipts shipped without considering their use as tools of coercive control.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

In 2018, it emerged that Cambridge Analytica had harvested data from 87 million Facebook users via a third-party quiz app. Facebook's privacy settings technically permitted this under their policies at the time. The lesson: compliant privacy architecture can still produce catastrophic privacy violations when data-sharing norms are designed to maximize data flow rather than user protection. <sup>9, 10</sup>

**Connections**

Privacy intersects with Security (technical protection of minimally collected data), Safety (surveillance features as safety risks), Autonomy (right to control one's own data), Economic Justice (data as extraction rather than service), and Belonging (whether users can participate without surrendering privacy).

**For the Builder**

Apply privacy-by-design: privacy architects or designated privacy reviewers must be involved in feature design from the start, not consulted after features are built. Privacy review should be a development gate. Conduct regular third-party privacy audits, internal review alone is insufficient.


### 7.  Security


    *The system protects user data and system integrity from unauthorized access, exploitation, and breach; security is a prerequisite for any system that handles user information, not a feature to be added post-launch.*

**Severity: Foundational**

**Intent**

Security and Safety are distinct obligations that require distinct design responses. Safety addresses harms within the system. Security addresses harms caused by the system being breached or exploited. Users who share data with a product have extended trust; a security failure is a breach of that trust with real-world consequences, financial fraud, identity theft, physical danger for users whose location data is exposed. Security failures compound inequity: users least equipped to manage the consequences of a data breach bear disproportionate harm.

Key obligations include: authentication and authorization (users are who they claim to be; they access only what they are permitted to access); encryption (data in transit and at rest); vulnerability management (a documented process for identifying and remediating security vulnerabilities, including responsible disclosure); supply chain security (third-party libraries and APIs are the product team's responsibility); and incident response (a tested plan that prioritizes user protection over reputational management).

**In Practice**



* Implement multi-factor authentication for accounts handling sensitive data.
* Encrypt all data in transit (TLS 1.2 minimum) and at rest.
* Conduct penetration testing before major launches and at regular intervals.
* Maintain a responsible disclosure program (bug bounty or coordinated disclosure).
* Audit third-party dependencies for known vulnerabilities on a regular schedule.
* Apply the principle of least privilege: users and systems access only what they need.
* Store passwords using modern cryptographic hashing (bcrypt, Argon2). Never plain text or MD5.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Security as a later sprint: Deferring security architecture decisions until the product "has sensitive data", by which point insecure architecture is deeply embedded.
* Security through obscurity: Relying on attackers not knowing about vulnerabilities rather than fixing them.
* Legal threats against researchers: Using legal mechanisms to suppress vulnerability disclosures rather than remediate them.
* Third-party blindness: Treating security as limited to code the team wrote, ignoring the risk surface of libraries, APIs, and integrations.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

The 2017 Equifax breach exposed personal data of 147 million people through a known vulnerability in the Apache Struts library that had been unpatched for over two months after a patch was available. The failure was not sophisticated: it was a documented vulnerability in a third-party component that the team was not tracking. Supply chain security is not optional. <sup>11, 12</sup>

**Connections**

Security intersects with Privacy (secure systems protect minimally collected data), Safety (security breaches create physical safety risks for exposed users), and Labor Ethics (security work must be resourced, under-resourced security teams produce insecure products).

**For the Builder**

Security must be resourced as infrastructure, not as a feature. Allocate dedicated security engineering time. Establish threat modeling as a standard part of feature design. Create a culture where raising security concerns is rewarded, not treated as blocker behavior.


### 8.  Safety


    *The system designs to prevent and mitigate psychological, emotional, mental, and physical harm to its users; proactively protects vulnerable populations; and provides accessible, effective means for users to seek help, report harm, and obtain remedy when the system fails.*

**Severity: Foundational**

**Intent**

A product is not neutral. Its design choices constitute a continuous series of decisions about who will be protected and from what. The safety obligation is not symmetric. Some users face orders of magnitude more risk from system failures than others: women and marginalized groups face disproportionate online harassment; LGBTQ+ users face targeted harassment and, in some contexts, physical risk from exposure; domestic violence survivors can be endangered by location features; people in mental health crises can be harmed by algorithmically amplified distressing content; children are vulnerable to predatory contact and inappropriate content.

AI systems create new safety categories that require explicit design responses: deepfakes and non-consensual synthetic media; AI-generated content that deceives users about its origin; automated decision systems with no human oversight pathway; and large language models that can generate dangerous information or target vulnerable users. The post-incident obligation is equally important: when users are harmed, the system must provide accessible reporting, responsive enforcement, and meaningful remedy.

**In Practice**



* Implement accessible, visible reporting mechanisms for harassment, threats, and self-harm violations, not buried in settings menus.
* Establish and publish response time commitments for safety reports.
* Design crisis pathways for users in mental health crises or experiencing domestic violence: surface external resources without requiring navigation of complex menus.
* Conduct pre-launch safety threat modeling specifically for vulnerable populations.
* Review content-surfacing algorithms for potential to amplify harmful content to vulnerable users.
* Apply AI content disclosure: users must know when they are interacting with AI-generated content or AI-mediated decisions.
* Maintain human review pathways for safety-critical decisions, no purely automated system should be the final arbiter.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Safety as moderation afterthought: Content moderation under-resourced relative to product growth, treated as a cost center rather than a safety function.
* The appeal process that doesn't work: A moderation appeal mechanism technically available but practically inaccessible or unresponded to.
* Vulnerable population blindness: Safety design that tests against a normative user while never modeling the experience of users at highest risk.
* Algorithm as safety tool: Relying on automated systems as the sole safety mechanism without human oversight of high-stakes decisions.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

Internal Facebook research published as part of the Facebook Papers (2021) documented that Instagram's recommendation algorithms were pushing eating disorder and self-harm content to teenage girls in a feedback loop the team identified and did not prioritize addressing. The safety failure was not ignorance, it was a resource allocation decision. <sup>13</sup>

**Connections**

Safety intersects with Belonging (psychological safety as a precondition), Privacy (surveillance features as safety risks), User Health (mental health as a safety dimension), Algorithmic Accountability (recommendation systems as safety-critical infrastructure), and Civic Responsibility (safety failures at scale become public health issues).

**For the Builder**

Safety must be treated as infrastructure. Fund safety teams proportionally to product scale. Include trust and safety specialists from communities at elevated risk. Test safety features with users from high-risk groups. Conduct pre-launch safety reviews as a development gate, not a post-launch audit.


### 9.  Representation & Stigma


    *The system does not perpetuate, amplify, or introduce harmful representations of people; it actively examines its own language, imagery, defaults, and content for dehumanizing or stigmatizing patterns, and maintains a process for identifying and correcting them.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

Stigma in product design is more often a sin of omission than commission. The product that never depicts disabled people, or depicts them only in the context of their disability. The health application that uses clinical language for mental illness but warm language for physical illness. The AI image system that defaults to white, male, Western faces for "CEO" and non-Western faces for "criminal." The form that requires users to select from two gender options, invisibilizing non-binary people. These are not aggressive acts, they are accumulated defaults that, in aggregate, communicate clearly about whose existence the system normalizes and whose it marginalizes.

The stigma obligation has four components: language stigma (dehumanizing copy, pathologizing language, or reducing people to their demographic characteristics); visual representation (who appears in illustrations, stock imagery, and generated content); default stigma (what normalcy assumptions are baked into form fields, data structures, and content models); and content amplification (whether the system's algorithms surface stigmatizing content at disproportionate rates).

**In Practice**



* Conduct a language audit of all system copy, including error messages, onboarding, help documentation, against an inclusive language guide.
* Review all illustrations, avatars, stock imagery, and AI-generated visual content for representation breadth.
* Audit all form fields for binary or exclusionary assumptions: gender, relationship status, name format, family structure.
* Establish a user-facing process for reporting stigmatizing language or representation with a commitment to review and respond.
* Test AI-generated content systems for demographic default biases before launch and at each major model update.

**Anti-Patterns**



* The token image: Adding one non-white face to a hero image while leaving all other visual representation unchanged.
* "Normal" as default: Forms and profiles that treat specific demographic characteristics, binary gender, Western name format, two-parent family, as the unmarked normal.
* AI generation without bias testing: Deploying image or text generation systems without auditing for demographic default patterns.
* Stigmatizing help copy: Using clinical or dehumanizing language in support documentation about sensitive topics, addiction, mental health, disability.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

Multiple independent studies have documented that AI image generation systems prompted with neutral terms like "CEO," "doctor," and "criminal" produce significantly skewed demographic representations reflecting historical data biases, not the actual demographics of those roles. These systems were deployed without adequate representation auditing. <sup>14</sup>

**Connections**

Representation & Stigma intersects with Belonging (representation as a belonging signal), Diversity (who appears in product content), Honesty & Truth (accurate vs. distorted representation), Algorithmic Accountability (recommendation systems amplifying stigmatizing content), and Safety (dehumanizing representation as a harassment vector).

**For the Builder**

Establish a living inclusive language guide for the product, reviewed and updated at least annually. Include representation review as part of the design critique process. Audit AI systems for demographic default biases before they ship and at each major model update.


### 10.  Algorithmic Accountability


    *The system is transparent about how algorithmic decisions are made, enables users to understand and contest decisions that affect them, and proactively tests for and remediates disparate outcomes across demographic groups.*

**Severity: Foundational (for consequential decisions); Structural (for recommendation and content systems)**

**Intent**

An algorithm is a policy. When a hiring tool decides whose resume is surfaced, a credit system decides who receives a loan, a content moderation system decides whose post is removed, or a recommendation engine decides what a user sees, these decisions affect people's lives, livelihoods, safety, and opportunities. The fact that they are made by software does not reduce their consequence or eliminate the obligation to make them fairly, transparently, and accountably.

Algorithmic accountability is about three things: transparency (users can understand how decisions that affect them are made); contestability (users have a meaningful path to challenge those decisions); and auditability (the system is subject to independent review for disparate outcomes). Key harm types: training data bias (models trained on historical data perpetuate historical discrimination); feedback loop amplification (biased outputs generate biased training data); proxy discrimination (facially neutral variables can discriminate by protected characteristic); disparate impact (discriminatory outcomes without discriminatory intent); and opacity of consequence (users who are algorithmically penalized often do not know it has happened).

**In Practice**



* For consequential algorithmic systems (hiring, credit, criminal justice, healthcare, content enforcement): provide plain-language decision explanations and a meaningful human review pathway.
* Conduct disparate impact testing before launch and at regular intervals: are outcomes systematically different across demographic groups?
* Apply EU AI Act high-risk AI system standards as an ethical baseline regardless of legal jurisdiction.
* Maintain a model card for all machine learning systems: what it does, what data it was trained on, known limitations, and demographic performance differentials.
* Do not deploy automated systems as the final arbiter of safety-critical decisions, maintain human oversight.

**Anti-Patterns**



* The black box defense: Using algorithmic complexity as justification for opacity: "our model is too complex to explain."
* Disparate impact ignorance: Launching algorithmic systems without testing for differential outcomes across demographic groups.
* Automated final decisions: Consequential decisions with no human review pathway.
* Model cards as compliance theater: Producing documentation for regulatory purposes without genuinely engaging with the limitations documented.
* Audit resistance: Designing systems that make independent algorithmic auditing technically or legally difficult.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

Amazon's experimental AI hiring tool (discontinued 2018) was trained on a decade of submitted resumes. Because the tech industry had historically been male-dominated, the model learned to penalize resumes that included the word "women's" and to downgrade graduates of all-women's colleges. Historical discrimination was encoded into automated hiring decisions. The system was discovered internally and discontinued, but the failure illustrates how unchecked training data propagates bias forward at scale. <sup>15</sup>

**Connections**

Algorithmic Accountability intersects with Equity (algorithmic systems perpetuate structural inequity at scale), Representation & Stigma (algorithmic amplification of stigmatizing content), Honesty & Truth (transparency about decision-making as an honesty obligation), Privacy (training data as a privacy concern), and Civic Responsibility (at scale, algorithmic systems shape public discourse and democratic outcomes).

**For the Builder**

Algorithmic systems should have named owners accountable for their social outcomes, not just their technical performance. Establish regular bias and disparate impact reviews. Maintain model documentation that is honest about limitations. Build independent audit access into system architecture.


### 11.  User Health


    *The system respects and supports the physical, psychological, and behavioral health of its users; it does not employ persuasive technology techniques to override user judgment, manufacture artificial needs, or optimize for engagement at the expense of wellbeing.*

**Severity: Foundational (for deliberate manipulation); Structural (for systemic design patterns)**

**Intent**

The attention economy has produced an industry-wide conflict of interest between product engagement metrics and user health. A product that measures its success by daily active users, time-on-platform, and notification click-through rate has built a business model around maximizing the time users spend engaged. User health requires the opposite: that users engage for the time that serves them, and disengage when continuing would not.

This is a measurable structural conflict, and the designers of engagement-maximization systems often know it. Variable reward mechanisms, infinite scroll, streak mechanics, and carefully timed notifications are deliberate applications of behavioral psychology, developed with full knowledge of their effects. The full user health obligation extends to: mental health effects (social comparison, filter bubbles, cyberbullying infrastructure, self-harm content amplification); behavioral design patterns (variable reward, infinite scroll, loss aversion manufacturing, notification manipulation); specific population vulnerabilities (children and adolescents, people with eating disorders, people with gambling disorders, elderly users in isolation); and the right to disengage (making it genuinely possible to stop using the product).

**In Practice**



* Provide genuine, accessible usage dashboards showing session length and frequency.
* Implement meaningful 'take a break' features, not performative ones that dismiss after one tap.
* Do not use variable reward mechanisms without disclosure and user control over their activation.
* Remove or make optional streak mechanics that generate loss aversion rather than intrinsic motivation.
* Establish a notification policy: when and why notifications are sent, optimized for user value, not re-engagement.
* Test content surfaces with users who have been consuming distressing content for extended periods: what does the system show them next?
* Provide accessible pathways to external support resources for users showing signs of mental health crisis or disordered behavior patterns.
* Apply age-appropriate design for products accessible to minors.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Engagement as a proxy for value: Measuring product success by time-on-platform without measuring whether that time benefited users.
* Infinite scroll: Removing natural stopping points from content feeds, an explicit design decision to extend session length by eliminating the opportunity to pause.
* Algorithmic suffering amplification: Recommendation systems that, having identified a user consuming distressing content, serve more distressing content because it produces engagement.
* Loss aversion manufacturing: Streak systems and "you've lost your streak" notifications designed to generate anxiety as an engagement driver.
* Notification manipulation: Sending notifications at times identified through behavioral research as maximizing re-engagement, regardless of whether the content is time-sensitive or valuable.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

In 2006, Aza Raskin invented infinite scroll. In subsequent years, as the behavioral evidence for its addictive properties accumulated, he publicly expressed regret, estimating the feature generates approximately 200,000 additional hours of collective daily scrolling across platforms. He has described it as a design he would not have shipped had he understood its behavioral consequences. The designer knew. The business decision continued. <sup>16, 17</sup>

**Connections**

User Health intersects with Safety (mental health crises as safety events), Autonomy & Agency (user's right to meaningful control over engagement), Economic Justice (engagement manipulation as revenue extraction), Civic Responsibility (attention economy at scale as a public health issue), and Honesty & Truth (disclosure of persuasive design techniques).

**For the Builder**

Conduct a persuasive technology audit of the product: identify every feature that uses behavioral psychology to influence engagement. For each, document what it does, why it exists, whether users are aware of it, and whether they can control it. Establish a health metric alongside engagement metrics: does user satisfaction and voluntary return rate support the inference that engagement is beneficial?


### 12.  Autonomy & Agency


    *The system enables users to make meaningful, informed choices about their experience; it does not use psychological manipulation to override user judgment; and it provides users with genuine, accessible control over their data, settings, and participation.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

Autonomy is the foundation beneath several other principles in this framework. Privacy requires meaningful consent to data collection. User Health requires the ability to disengage from persuasive design. Belonging requires ability to configure community experience. All of these collapse without genuine user autonomy, the right of users to make meaningful choices that the system actually honors.

The autonomy obligation is violated not only by obvious manipulation but by friction design: making choices the system prefers easy, and choices it doesn't prefer difficult. When canceling a subscription requires a 45-minute retention chat and multiple declines, the system has made a choice about whose interests it serves. When accepting all cookies is one click and declining is nine steps through nested menus, the system has made a privacy choice on the user's behalf while maintaining the fiction of consent. Key dimensions: informed defaults (defaults must be aligned with user interests, not platform interests); genuine configurability (substantive, not cosmetic, control); freedom from manipulation (no psychological coercion to override expressed preferences); right to leave (data export in usable formats, account deletion without unreasonable barriers); and AI oversight (human review pathway for consequential AI decisions).

**In Practice**



* Document every default setting with explicit rationale: what user interest does it serve?
* Provide a settings dashboard presenting the most impactful privacy and engagement choices prominently, not buried in nested menus.
* Account and data deletion must be accessible within a small number of clearly labeled steps.
* Data export must be available in portable, usable formats.
* Provide granular control over algorithmic personalization: users must be able to adjust, reduce, or disable recommendation systems.
* For AI-influenced consequential decisions, provide plain-language explanation and a human review pathway.

**Anti-Patterns**



* The roach motel: Easy to enter, difficult to leave. Sign-up takes 30 seconds; account deletion requires finding a buried link, confirming through email, waiting 30 days, and declining multiple retention offers.
* Default maximalism: Defaulting to maximum data sharing and notification frequency, framed as "we've enabled everything for your best experience."
* Dark settings UX: Interface design that makes privacy-protective choices physically smaller, less prominent, or more navigational steps away than permissive choices.
* Algorithmic opacity: Recommendation systems with no user-facing controls, presented as fixed features rather than configurable systems.
* Artificial urgency: "This offer expires in 10 minutes" countdown timers that reset on page refresh.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

The FTC's 2023 enforcement actions against multiple major companies for dark patterns, hidden subscription fees, misleading cancellation flows, and unauthorized charges, established these practices as legally actionable deceptions under Section 5. Products that design deception into their monetization flows are not being commercially clever; they are building legal liability into their products. <sup>18</sup>

**Connections**

Autonomy & Agency intersects with Privacy (meaningful consent requires genuine autonomy), User Health (autonomy as the right to disengage), False Obsolescence (data portability as an autonomy right), Honesty & Truth (manipulation as deception), and Economic Justice (autonomy violations that serve platform revenue over user interest).

**For the Builder**

Apply a defaults audit: for every default setting, document what it is and whose interests it primarily serves. Establish a dark patterns review as part of the design critique process, specifically evaluate monetization flows, cancellation flows, and consent mechanisms. Involve users from diverse backgrounds in testing control features: are they genuinely discoverable and usable?


### 13.  Honesty & Truth


    *The system does not deceive users through its design, language, or content; it discloses algorithmic curation and AI-generated content; it does not enable or amplify the spread of false information; and it respects users' epistemic autonomy, their capacity to form independent, well-informed judgments.*

**Severity: Foundational (for dark patterns and AI disclosure); Structural (for misinformation and epistemic autonomy)**

**Intent**

Honesty in product design encompasses four distinct obligations. First, honesty in design: dark patterns, deceptive UX including hidden costs, manipulative cancellation flows, confirmshaming ("No thanks, I prefer to stay uninformed"), and misleading layout, are designed dishonesty. They violate this principle regardless of whether the content is factually accurate. Second, algorithmic transparency: when recommendations benefit the platform commercially and not the user, this must be disclosed. Third, AI content disclosure: synthetic media produced or deployed without disclosure is a form of deception regardless of content; this is becoming a legal standard (EU AI Act) and is an ethical standard everywhere. Fourth, epistemic autonomy: algorithmic curation that creates false impressions of consensus, normalizes fringe views, or suppresses diverse perspectives is a honesty violation even in the absence of any false statement, it degrades users' capacity to form accurate, independent judgments about the world.

**In Practice**



* Conduct a dark patterns audit of every monetization flow, cancellation flow, and consent mechanism against deceptivedesign.org taxonomy and EU Digital Services Act standards.
* Disclose all sponsored, affiliated, or platform-owned content in search results, recommendations, and feeds.
* Implement AI content labeling for all user-facing AI-generated text, images, audio, and video.
* Provide clear, plain-language explanation of how content recommendation systems work.
* Evaluate recommendation systems for the types of content they disproportionately surface.
* Write all consumer-facing legal documents in plain language. If it requires a lawyer to understand, it does not constitute informed consent.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Confirmshaming: Opt-out language written to shame the user: "No thanks, I don't want to save money."
* The manufactured countdown: Urgency timers that reset on page refresh, artificial scarcity as a purchase driver.
* Undisclosed sponsorship: Recommendations commercially influenced without disclosure.
* AI ghost-writing without disclosure: Deploying AI-generated support responses, marketing content, or journalistic copy as if human-authored.
* The unreadable terms: A 15,000-word terms of service document written to protect the platform from liability while technically satisfying the informed consent requirement.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

The FTC's 2023 crackdown on dark patterns resulted in major settlements for hidden subscription fees, misleading cancellation flows, and unauthorized charges. Dark patterns are explicitly named by the FTC as deceptive practices subject to Section 5 enforcement, they are not merely ethical violations but legally actionable deceptions that have generated hundreds of millions of dollars in regulatory penalties. <sup>22</sup>

**Connections**

Honesty & Truth intersects with Autonomy & Agency (manipulation as autonomy violation), User Health (persuasive design disclosure), Algorithmic Accountability (transparency about decision-making), Privacy (plain-language consent as an honesty requirement), Civic Responsibility (misinformation at scale as a civic harm), and Representation & Stigma (honest representation of social reality).

**For the Builder**

Conduct a dark patterns review of every commercial and consent flow. Use external reviewers, internal teams are desensitized to the patterns they designed. Apply an AI content disclosure standard before any generative AI features ship. Establish a plain language standard for all user-facing legal and policy documents.


### 14.  False Obsolescence


    *The system does not artificially shorten its own useful life, manufacture user dependency that traps users, or design features or hardware to fail or become obsolete before their natural end of life.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

False obsolescence is the engineering of impermanence in the service of revenue. In hardware, it is the documented Phoebus cartel pattern. In software, it is subtler but pervasive: forced updates that remove relied-upon functionality; API deprecation with inadequate notice; subscription tiers that gradually absorb features that were previously included; file formats designed to make migration costly; and platform abandonment, building user dependency, then killing the product.

The software-specific taxonomy includes: forced deprecation (platforms killed with 90 days' notice after building genuine user dependency, over 250 documented Google product terminations as of 2024); feature regression disguised as improvement (removing advanced features framed as "simplification"); artificial subscription barriers (bait-and-switch from free to paid tiers); format lock-in (proprietary formats that make competitor migration prohibitive); right to repair (hardware designed to resist owner repair through proprietary fasteners, software locks, and legal threats against independent repair shops); and environmental cascade (each device driven to premature disposal generates e-waste, the global e-waste stream is the fastest-growing waste category).

**In Practice**



* Establish and publish product lifecycle commitments before launch: minimum active development duration and minimum deprecation notice period.
* Provide data export in open, portable formats at all times, not only when the product is being discontinued.
* Communicate feature changes transparently and with sufficient lead time for users to adapt.
* Apply the Right to Repair principle: for hardware products, use standard fasteners, publish repair documentation, and do not use software locks to void warranties for owner repairs.
* Evaluate API deprecation policies for adequacy of migration timeline.
* Do not move features from included tiers to paid tiers without explicit, honest communication of the change.

**Anti-Patterns**



* The Google Graveyard launch: Building user dependency on a product with no stated commitment to maintenance, then discontinuing it with minimal notice.
* "Simplification" regressions: Removing relied-upon features under the framing of design improvement.
* Subscription bait-and-switch: A free or low-cost product that gradually moves features behind higher-tier paywalls until the original product no longer exists.
* Proprietary lock-in: File formats and export restrictions designed to make migration to competitors technically costly.
* Performance throttling: Deliberately reducing device performance through software updates to encourage hardware replacement, without user knowledge or consent.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

In 2017, Apple acknowledged that iOS updates intentionally throttled performance on older iPhones to manage battery degradation. The technical rationale was legitimate. The violation was absence of disclosure: users experiencing slower phones after updates had no way to know this was designed behavior rather than hardware failure. Multiple countries investigated and fined Apple for this practice. <sup>19, 20</sup>

**Connections**

False Obsolescence intersects with Autonomy & Agency (data portability as an autonomy right), Environmental Sustainability (planned obsolescence as an environmental harm), Economic Justice (lock-in as an extraction mechanism), Privacy (right to erasure requires data deletion capability), and Honesty & Truth (undisclosed deprecation as deception).

**For the Builder**

Establish an explicit product lifecycle policy before launch and publish it. Before any major platform change or deprecation, conduct a user impact assessment: what workflows and data will be disrupted, and what migration support will be provided? Make data portability a first-class engineering concern, not an afterthought.


### 15.  Economic Justice


    *The system does not exploit information asymmetry, psychological vulnerability, or market power to extract disproportionate value from users; it is transparent about its commercial incentives; and it does not engineer financial harm as a product feature.*

**Severity: Foundational (for predatory features); Structural (for information asymmetry and extraction design)**

**Intent**

Products exist within economic systems, and their design reflects economic incentives. Under a shareholder-primacy model, a product that can generate more revenue by exploiting user psychology, obscuring true costs, or leveraging market power to trap users has a structural incentive to do so. Economic justice as a product principle names this conflict and requires it be resolved in favor of users, not the platform.

Cory Doctorow's concept of "enshittification", the documented lifecycle in which platforms build value for users, then degrade that value to extract revenue once users are locked in, describes the predictable outcome of shareholder-primacy economics applied to two-sided markets. Key harm types: predatory monetization (loot boxes, gacha mechanics, free-to-play designs targeting compulsive behavior, BNPL interfaces that obscure true credit costs); information asymmetry (using behavioral data to price and negotiate in ways that consistently favor the platform); true cost opacity (the full data, attention, and behavioral cost of "free" products must be disclosed); and market power abuse (using platform dominance to degrade product quality once switching costs are established).

**In Practice**



* Audit all monetization flows against the FTC's dark patterns guidance and the EU Digital Services Act.
* Disclose the behavioral data cost of 'free' products explicitly and prominently.
* Do not use personalized behavioral profiles to set prices without disclosure.
* Gambling-adjacent mechanics must include: odds disclosure, spending limits, easy opt-out, and specific consideration of whether minors can access them.
* Make subscription cancellation as frictionless as sign-up.
* Apply CFPB guidance to any product that enables or facilitates consumer credit, BNPL, or financial product integration.

**Anti-Patterns**



* The freemium trap: A product with no monetary cost that extracts user value through behavioral profiling and data brokering without disclosure.
* Gambling mechanics without disclosure: Variable reward systems with real-money exchange that do not disclose odds or apply spending protections.
* Retention hostage-taking: Making account closure, data export, or subscription cancellation artificially difficult to preserve revenue from users who have decided to leave.
* Personalized price discrimination: Using behavioral data to charge higher prices to users identified as less price-sensitive, without disclosure.
* Enshittification cycle: Building user dependency through genuine value, then systematically degrading quality and increasing extraction once switching costs are established.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

The UK's Competition and Markets Authority 2022 investigation into mobile gaming monetization documented predatory free-to-play mechanics that specifically targeted compulsive behavior patterns and children's underdeveloped impulse control, generating revenue from users identified as most vulnerable to the mechanics, not most capable of affording them. <sup>23</sup>

**Connections**

Economic Justice intersects with Autonomy & Agency (manipulation and lock-in as autonomy violations), User Health (predatory monetization as a health harm), False Obsolescence (lock-in as an economic justice issue), Honesty & Truth (undisclosed commercial incentives as deception), and Civic Responsibility (economic concentration enabled by extractive design as a democratic concern).

**For the Builder**

Establish an explicit commercial ethics policy: what monetization approaches will and will not be used, and why. Apply this as a design constraint, not a post-hoc review. Ensure the product team understands the full economic cost structure of the product from the user's perspective, not just the price, but the data, attention, and behavioral costs.


### 16.  Environmental Sustainability


    *The system minimizes its ecological footprint across its full lifecycle, from the energy cost of computation to the materials cost of hardware to the waste generated at end of life, and does not make false or misleading claims about its environmental impact.*

**Severity: Structural**

**Intent**

Digital products are not immaterial. Every computation has an energy cost. Every model training run has a carbon footprint. Every data center requires cooling and power. Every device requires materials that must be mined, refined, and manufactured. Every obsoleted device becomes waste. The environmental footprint of the technology sector is large, growing, and significantly underreported relative to other industries.

This principle does not require minimizing utility to minimize environmental impact. It requires that environmental impact be treated as a real cost weighed in design decisions, not externalized to future generations or geographically distant communities who bear the consequences without sharing the benefits. Key dimensions: computational efficiency (AI systems have energy costs that scale with intensity; choose the smallest model that accomplishes the task); hardware lifecycle (design for repairability, longevity, and recyclability); infrastructure choices (renewable-powered regions and providers are available choices); e-waste (the global e-waste stream is the fastest-growing waste category, every product that drives premature device replacement contributes directly); and greenwashing (carbon neutrality claims must reflect genuine measured reduction, not offset accounting that displaces rather than eliminates harm).

**In Practice**



* Calculate and disclose the energy and carbon footprint of the product's primary operations.
* Select infrastructure providers with credible renewable energy commitments.
* Optimize AI model deployment for efficiency: use the smallest model that accomplishes the task.
* Apply the False Obsolescence principle to hardware products: design for longevity, repairability, and end-of-life recyclability.
* Review all environmental claims against FTC Green Guides standards before publication.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Carbon offset theater: Claiming carbon neutrality through purchased offsets while making no reductions in actual emissions.
* The "sustainable" label without evidence: Environmental marketing claims unsupported by measurement or certification.
* AI maximalism: Deploying the largest, most compute-intensive model available regardless of whether a smaller model would accomplish the task.
* Greenwashing through omission: Highlighting user-facing environmental choices while obscuring the platform's own significantly larger footprint.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

The training run for GPT-3 was estimated to consume approximately 1,287 MWh of electricity and generate approximately 552 tonnes of CO2 equivalent. As AI model sizes have increased, energy costs have scaled accordingly, without proportional transparency about these costs in product environmental disclosures. This is not a call to halt AI development; it is a call to measure and disclose its real costs.<sup>24, 25</sup>

**Connections**

Environmental Sustainability intersects with False Obsolescence (planned obsolescence as an environmental harm), Labor Ethics (rare earth mining and electronics manufacturing conditions), Economic Justice (environmental costs externalized to communities with less market power), and Honesty & Truth (greenwashing as deception).

**For the Builder**

Assign environmental ownership: who on the team is responsible for tracking and reducing the product's environmental footprint? Incorporate energy cost and carbon footprint into evaluation criteria for major infrastructure and model deployment decisions. Treat environmental impact as a product quality metric.


### 17.  Labor Ethics


    *The system's ethical responsibility extends to the people who build, operate, and support it; the team behind the product has the same claim to dignity, safety, and fair compensation as the users in front of it.*

**Severity: Foundational**

**Intent**

A product designed to protect user wellbeing while exposing its workers to harm, exploitation, or unsafe conditions has not taken the ethical turn it claims. The labor ethics of a product are part of its ethical footprint, inseparable from the user ethics the product claims to embody.

The full labor ethics landscape: content moderation labor (human moderators reviewing graphic violence, child exploitation, and extreme content at high volume, frequently contractors, paid below equivalent employee rates, with limited psychological support and less job security, while a product provides crisis support to users experiencing distressing content); AI training labor (a global workforce largely in Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America that labels training data and performs human-in-the-loop tasks, documented at sub-minimum wages with exposure to deeply disturbing content); gig and contractor labor (workers whose safety, compensation, and working conditions are directly affected by product design decisions regardless of employment classification); builder diversity and safety (the team itself is entitled to a workplace that is diverse, inclusive, equitable, and psychologically safe, a team that fails its own members will reproduce those failures in its product); and supply chain (hardware products have supply chains with documented labor rights issues).

**In Practice**



* For products with content moderation at scale: establish maximum exposure limits, mandatory psychological support, rotation policies, and fair compensation comparable to in-house product employees.
* For products using AI training data services: conduct supply chain audits of labeling vendors; establish and apply fair compensation and working conditions standards in vendor contracts.
* For products using gig labor: disclose pay rates, apply safety standards, and provide fair dispute resolution mechanisms.
* Establish a builder team diversity target and measure it as a product quality metric.
* Apply the same psychological safety standards to the product team as the product applies to its users.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Outsourced ethics: Contracting content moderation, data labeling, and gig labor through intermediaries that insulate the product team from direct responsibility for the conditions of that labor.
* Contractor two-tier: Full-time employees with full benefits and psychological support working alongside contractors performing equivalent or more difficult work under significantly inferior conditions.
* Diversity as PR: Publishing diversity statistics as marketing while failing to address structural inclusion failures on the team itself.
* Silence on supply chain: Hardware products that make no inquiry into the labor conditions of their component supply chains.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

A 2023 TIME investigation documented that Kenyan workers labeling content for OpenAI's ChatGPT were paid approximately $2 per hour to review graphic content including descriptions of violence, sexual abuse, and torture. The workers reported significant psychological harm. This is not an anomaly, it is the documented baseline of much AI training data labor. It is the supply chain of the AI industry, operating in the ethical blind spot of most product teams.<sup>26</sup>

**Connections**

Labor Ethics intersects with Diversity (diverse builders as a product quality obligation), Equity (fair compensation across the builder team), Belonging (psychological safety for builders), Safety (content moderation worker safety), and Environmental Sustainability (electronics supply chain as a shared labor and environmental issue).

**For the Builder**

This principle is uniquely self-referential: the builder is the subject. Conduct an honest assessment of the working conditions, diversity, pay equity, and psychological safety of the product team, including contractors and vendors, using the same rigor you would apply to any other design audit.


### 18.  Civic Responsibility


    *The system acknowledges and takes responsibility for its aggregate effects on democratic institutions, social cohesion, and public discourse; it does not design for engagement in ways that predictably corrode civic trust; and it does not enable the weaponization of its infrastructure against democratic participation or civic safety.*

**Severity: Structural (for aggregate effects); Foundational (for direct democratic or civil liberties harms)**

**Intent**

Products that reach millions of users are not merely consumer products, they are civic infrastructure. The design decisions of a social platform affect the quality of public discourse. The algorithm of a news aggregator affects what populations consider shared reality. The moderation policies of a messaging platform affect whether political organizing is possible for minority communities. At sufficient scale, product design decisions are not merely user experience choices, they are choices about the health of democratic society.

This does not mean product teams must solve political polarization. It means they must not engineer those outcomes in pursuit of engagement metrics, and they must design thoughtfully for aggregate civic consequences. Key dimensions: radicalization architecture (recommendation systems optimized for engagement on platforms hosting political content have been documented to systematically amplify extreme content); polarization amplification (outrage-optimized curation maximizes engagement and accelerates political polarization, degrading the capacity for democratic deliberation); election integrity (specific obligations during election periods regarding disinformation and suppression); surveillance enablement (products usable by states to persecute their users have an obligation to design against this use case); and institutional trust (treatment of authoritative sources, scientific institutions, journalism, public health agencies, affects public trust at aggregate scale).

**In Practice**



* Conduct a civic impact assessment for recommendation and content-surfacing algorithms: what is the aggregate effect on viewpoint diversity users encounter?
* Establish specific protocols for election periods: enhanced misinformation monitoring, reduced amplification of contested political claims, increased transparency about political advertising.
* Evaluate the product's potential for surveillance misuse in authoritarian contexts.
* Apply the EU Digital Services Act Very Large Platform standards as an ethical benchmark regardless of legal requirement.
* Audit the treatment of authoritative sources in recommendation and content systems.

**Anti-Patterns**



* Engagement maximization without civic impact assessment: Optimizing recommendation systems for engagement without evaluating aggregate civic effects.
* "We're just a platform": Treating the system's effects on civic discourse as consequences of user choice rather than design decisions.
* Surveillance by API: Providing data access that enables mass surveillance of user behavior by state actors without meaningful access controls.
* Election emergency protocols that activate too late: Crisis protocols that only engage after harm is measurable, rather than proactively.

**Failure Modes in Practice**

In 2021, the Facebook Papers, internal research released by a whistleblower, showed that Facebook's own researchers had identified that recommendation algorithms were driving political polarization and recommending extremist groups to users who had engaged with moderate political content, and that executive leadership had repeatedly deprioritized fixes because they were tied to engagement metrics. The company knew its product was corroding civic discourse. It chose engagement. This is the defining civic responsibility failure mode of the current era: not ignorance of civic harm, but a business model that prices it as acceptable. <sup>27, 28</sup>

**Connections**

Civic Responsibility intersects with Algorithmic Accountability (recommendation systems as civic infrastructure), Honesty & Truth (misinformation and epistemic autonomy), User Health (polarization and outrage optimization as public mental health issues), Safety (at scale, civic harms become safety events), and Economic Justice (extractive engagement models as drivers of civic harm).

**For the Builder**

Establish a civic impact review for major algorithmic changes, equivalent to an environmental impact assessment. Define explicitly what the product will and will not do during election periods, and publish those commitments. Identify the highest-risk civic harm scenarios the product could enable, and design mitigations before those scenarios occur.


## From Principles to Practice

These eighteen principles are not a checklist. They are a framework for ongoing evaluation, a set of lenses through which any product decision can be examined for its ethical dimensions.

The hardest lesson from the history of DEI work, and from every ethical framework adopted by organizations that did not change, is this: principles that exist only as declarations produce no change. The organizations that have made measurable progress on equity, inclusion, and ethical product design are the ones that treated their commitments as design constraints with observable criteria and measures, not as values statements with admirable intent.

Applied in the spirit of this framework, these principles produce questions, not answers:



* Is this feature accessible to all of our users, or to the users it's easiest to design for?
* Whose data are we collecting, and what is the minimum we need?
* Does this default serve the user or the platform?
* Who is not in the room when this decision is being made, and what would they say?
* What is the worst reasonable thing someone could do with this feature, and what have we designed to prevent it?
* Five years from now, what will we wish we had thought of today?

The designer, product manager, developer, or business leader who asks these questions consistently, who creates space for them in critique and review, and who is willing to delay a ship date for the answers is not just building better products. They are building a practice of responsibility that the profession desperately needs.
